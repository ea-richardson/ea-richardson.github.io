Spatially Adjusted Correlations Between Crime and Topographic Variables: Relief, Roughness, and Distance to the Nearest Mountain

This study explores the spatially adjusted correlations between crime rates and three topographic variables—relief, roughness (elevation variability), and distance to the nearest mountain—within Los Angeles County, California. It introduces new approaches in environmental criminology and crime geography research, including a spatially-adjusted correlation test and a novel focus on distance to the nearest mountain. The study finds that the influence of topography on crime varies significantly across space, with certain regions, such as the Santa Monica Mountains and Malibu, showing notable correlations.

Tools: ArcGIS Pro, R
Data Sources: Los Angeles County Sheriff's Department, OpenStreetMap, U.S. Census Bureau, U.S. Geological Survey
